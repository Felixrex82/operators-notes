## The attention economy has been one of the most significant breakthroughs in the consumer internet over the past few years.

For the first time, users are no longer just the product, they're beginning to participate in the value they create. Brands can reach engaged audiences more directly, and consumers can finally be rewarded for the attention they contribute.
In many ways, it redefines the relationship between brands and users by reducing the dominance of traditional advertising platforms.

> "The true nature of the decentralized internet is to return power to the people who power the internet in the first place (i.e the users)."
— Felix Olamide

However, despite its promise, I believe the current generation of attention platforms has one fundamental weakness.

## Building on Someone Else's Foundation

Imagine Apple launching the iPhone 17 on DOS 3.0.

No matter how revolutionary the hardware was, it would always be constrained by an operating system it neither owned nor controlled.

That's how I view many of today's attention platforms.

** *Take Kaito as an example.* **
Rather than building a native attention infrastructure, it built an ecosystem largely around an existing social network—X (formerly Twitter).

While this approach accelerated adoption, it also inherited the limitations of the underlying platform.

The rules of distribution, identity, engagement, and content discovery are ultimately defined by X—not by the attention protocol itself.

As a result, the attention economy is still operating as a layer on top of Web2, rather than becoming a new layer of the internet.
And that distinction matters.

Because if the underlying platform changes its APIs, algorithms, pricing, or policies, the entire ecosystem built above it must adapt.

That's not sovereignty. It's dependency.
