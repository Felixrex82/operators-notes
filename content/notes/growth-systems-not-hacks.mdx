The term "growth hacking" should be retired.

Not because the tactics are always bad. Some of them work. The problem is the framing.

Hacks are one-time. Systems compound.

The companies that grow sustainably aren't running clever experiments in isolation. They're building growth functions — repeatable, measurable, improvable processes that deliver users and revenue reliably.

## What a Growth System Actually Is

A growth system has four components:

**Acquisition** — How do users discover you? What channels work, at what cost, with what quality?

**Activation** — How quickly do users experience the value that made them sign up? This is the most underinvested part of most growth functions.

**Retention** — Why do users come back? What's the habit, the value, the switching cost?

**Expansion** — How do existing users generate more revenue and more users over time?

The key word is *system*. Each component connects to the others. Improving activation doesn't just improve conversion — it improves retention, which improves word of mouth, which reduces acquisition cost.

## The Metric That Matters Most

Every growth system has one metric that, when it moves, moves everything else.

For a marketplace: liquidity (the probability that a search returns a satisfying result).  
For a communication tool: the number of connections a new user makes in their first week.  
For a content platform: the number of pieces of content a new user saves or engages with in session one.

Find this metric. It's not always obvious. Sometimes you have to work backward from your retention data to find what predicts a user becoming a long-term user.

Once you find it, build the entire activation experience around delivering that metric as quickly as possible.

## Why Most Growth Efforts Fail

Most growth efforts fail because they're disconnected from the product.

A growth team that operates separately from product is solving the wrong problem. Growth isn't something you spray on top of a product. It's something you build into it.

The best growth teams I've studied are essentially product teams with a specific charter: reduce the time between someone discovering your product and them experiencing the thing that makes them stay.

That's it. Everything else is in service of that.
