Most Web3 criticism focuses on the wrong things.

Critics point to speculation, scams, and energy consumption. Proponents point to decentralization, ownership, and composability. Both sides are talking past the actual problem.

The failure of most Web3 products is a product problem.

## Who Web3 Products Are Actually Built For

Spend 10 minutes with any "mainstream" Web3 app and the implicit user profile becomes obvious: someone who already has a wallet, understands gas fees, knows what a seed phrase is, and has a risk tolerance that would terrify their parents.

That's not a mainstream user. That's a narrow early adopter.

The products that will actually matter — that will onboard the next 100 million users — have to solve a different problem entirely. They have to make the underlying technology completely invisible.

The infrastructure can be decentralized. The experience cannot require users to understand why.

## The Abstraction Problem

Crypto-natives sometimes resist abstraction. "If users don't understand wallets, they're not really decentralized," goes the argument.

This is technically interesting and strategically suicidal.

The average person has no idea how VISA processes a transaction. They don't understand TCP/IP when they load a webpage. The sophistication of the underlying layer has never been a prerequisite for adoption.

What users need to understand: what they can do, what it costs, and what they get.

What users don't need to understand: consensus mechanisms, key management, or block finality.

## What Web3 Actually Gets Right

Despite the UX failures, there are genuine insights embedded in the Web3 thesis that I think are correct:

**Digital ownership will matter.** The generation growing up with in-game assets, digital collectibles, and online identities will demand that these assets are actually theirs. Not licensed. Owned.

**Programmable money is a genuine primitive.** The ability to build financial logic directly into value transfer — without an intermediary — is a new capability, not just a new interface.

**Credentialing on-chain is underrated.** The ability to verify achievements, contributions, and reputation without relying on a centralized issuer (a university, a company, a platform) is genuinely valuable and mostly unexplored.

## The Product Opportunity

The builders who will win in Web3 are the ones who work backward from user problems rather than forward from blockchain capabilities.

The question isn't "how do we put this on-chain?"

The question is "what does this user actually need, and is the chain the best tool to deliver it?"

Sometimes the answer is yes. Often, the answer is a hybrid. Occasionally, it's no.

The first wave of Web3 built infrastructure. The second wave is building abstractions. The third wave — the one that actually matters for mass adoption — will build products that work so well users forget what's underneath.

That wave hasn't started yet. That's the opportunity.
