The scarce resource in tech isn't capital. It isn't talent. And despite what the headlines say, it isn't compute.

It's attention.

Every product — whether it's a B2B SaaS tool, a consumer app, or a Web3 protocol — is now competing in the same fundamental market: the 24 hours each person has in a day.

## The Convergence Nobody Talks About

Something quietly happened in the last decade. The boundary between "media company" and "technology company" dissolved. Not because media went digital. Because every technology product had to develop a media function to survive.

Notion became a media company. They publish essays, YouTube content, and have a community layer that rivals editorial publications.

Stripe became a media company. Stripe Press, the annual report, the developer documentation — all of it is content designed to build the mental model that Stripe is the serious choice.

Linear became a media company. Their changelog reads like a magazine. Their team writes publicly about software philosophy.

This isn't marketing. This is product.

## What This Means for Builders

If you're building a product today, you need to answer one question before you write a single line of code:

*Why would someone give you their attention?*

Not their money. Not their data. Their time.

The companies that have figured this out are the ones that don't feel like products — they feel like places. Places people return to not because they have to, but because they want to.

## The Three Attention Models

After studying this across dozens of products, I've noticed three distinct models:

**1. The Utility Lock**  
Attention through necessity. Jira. Excel. Salesforce. People give you their attention because their job requires it. The danger: you can be displaced the moment a better utility appears.

**2. The Habit Loop**  
Attention through repetition. Twitter/X. Slack. Email. You've embedded yourself in the daily ritual. The advantage: powerful retention. The danger: one UX misstep can break the habit and users never return.

**3. The Identity Signal**  
Attention through aspiration. Linear. Figma. Arc. Using you signals something about who the user is or wants to be. This is the most defensible form of attention. Competitors can copy features. They can't copy identity.

## The Operator's Takeaway

Every product decision is an attention decision. When you add a feature, you're asking for more attention. When you simplify, you're being more respectful of it.

The products that win the next decade won't be the ones with the most features. They'll be the ones that made the most efficient use of the time users gave them.

Build accordingly.
