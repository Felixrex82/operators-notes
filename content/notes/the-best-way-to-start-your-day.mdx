 *The First Ten Minutes of Your Morning Decides the Rest of It.*

The alarm goes off but before your feet could even touch the floor, your hand is already reaching out for your phone. You didn't decide to do that. It just happens, and within seconds you're already scrolling — a message here, a headline there, someone's engagement photos, someone else's rant about fuel price, a WhatsApp forward you didn't ask for. 
Now, by the time you actually sit up, your mind is already full, with all sorts of information that has nothing to do with you but had already shapened how your day is going to be.

Here's the thing nobody tells you: that's not a neutral way to start the day because whatever gets into your head first thing in the morning doesn't just sit there quietly. It sets the mood you carry into everything else. You just don't usually notice it happening, because it happens before you're even fully awake.

![Image](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/felixrex82/operators-notes/main/public/uploads/1783931728222-bkqqvyc8fm9.jpg)

There's a reason the first few minutes of your day hit differently than any other part of it. Right after you wake up, your body goes through something called the cortisol awakening response — a natural spike in cortisol that happens in the first thirty to forty-five minutes after opening your eyes. Cortisol gets a bad reputation as "the stress hormone," but in this context it's actually what pulls your brain from asleep to alert, hence, your brain's ignition switch, however, it doesn't care what it's starting up — it just gets you moving, in whatever direction the first thing in front of you points.

This is exactly why the first input matters so much because your brain, at that moment, is unusually open — more alert than it was five minutes ago, but not yet cluttered with the day's actual demands. Whatever you then feed it first gets absorbed with very little resistance, because there's nothing else competing for space yet.

Most people's answer to "what's the first input" is a phone. A study by IDC, done with Facebook a few years back, found that eight out of ten smartphone users check their phone within fifteen minutes of waking up and it's not because they urgently need to but because it's there, and because apps are built deliberately — to reward you unpredictably enough that your brain keeps coming back to check, the same logic slot machines use. 

![Image](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/felixrex82/operators-notes/main/public/uploads/1783931987244-mgurf80ffoe.jpg)

Now, you see that it's not an accident. Somebody designed it that way, and it works exactly as intended.

So think about what that means, how that the very first thing touching your most receptive, least defended mental state of the entire day is usually whatever a notification algorithm decided to serve you — outrage, comparison, somebody else's highlight reel, a problem that isn't even yours yet and the funniest thing is, you didn't choose it, you were only a victim, in the fifteen minutes you were least equipped to filter it.

There's a concept researchers call attention residue. A researcher named Sophie Leroy studied this — she found that when you switch from one task to another, your brain doesn't fully leave the first task behind. Some part of your attention stays stuck there, quietly running in the background, even while you've moved on to something else. 
This is why you can close an argument on your phone and still feel the weight of it an hour later during a meeting that has nothing to do with it.

Now apply that to your morning. If the first thing your brain engages with is fifteen minutes of fragmented, emotionally loaded scrolling, that's exactly what leaves residue. 

You walk into your day already carrying somebody else's opinion, somebody else's bad news, somebody else's flex — and it colours how you react to the first real thing that happens to you that morning, even though it has nothing to do with any of it. It's not about the five minutes, it's about what those five minutes leave behind in a brain that was uniquely open to absorbing it.

Reading works completely different, and not just because it feels calmer but because it demands something scrolling never asks of you: sustained, single-threaded attention. 
You can't read a page while half-processing three other things the way you can while scrolling because your brain has to actually settle into one line of thought and follow it, which is a very different kind of mental exercise than the rapid, fragmented switching a feed trains you into.

That difference matters more in the morning than at any other time of day, because of what we already said — your brain is unusually receptive right after waking. If you feed it fragmentation first thing in the morning, you're training it to expect fragmentation and if you feed it something that requires actual focus, you're training that same absorbent brain to expect depth. 
You're not just choosing what information goes in, you're choosing what mode your brain defaults to for the rest of the day. This is also why the specific book barely matters as much as people think. Whether you're reading a chapter of a novel, a few pages of something on your industry, or a passage that makes you think, the content matters less than the shape of the activity itself. You're not just learning facts, you're rehearsing focus, first thing, before the day gets a chance to fragment you first.

![Image](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/felixrex82/operators-notes/main/public/uploads/1783932752654-k0h9rxvrhd.png)

**Here's the real shift reading in the morning creates:** 

It puts you in control of your own first move while on a scrolling morning, your day's tone gets set by whoever posted something engaging enough to catch your eye — a stranger, an algorithm, a group chat. Here, you didn't choose the agenda, you were only reactin to it.
But on a reading morning, you set the agenda yourself,deciding what enters your head first, which means you decided, in some small but real way, what frame you're going to see the rest of the day through. Someone who starts their morning with a chapter about discipline, or history, or their own field of work, walks into their 8am meeting carrying a completely different residue than someone who starts the morning absorbing an argument that has nothing to do with them. Same meeting but a different person walking into it.

Warren Buffett, famously, still reads for hours most days — reportedly around 500 pages, based on his own comments over the years and he has outrightly said that *reading compounds the same way interest does: not obvious day to day, but massive over years*. That's not really a productivity tip, it's the same point from a different angle. What goes into your head repeatedly, first, ends up shaping what kind of thinking you're capable of later.

![Image](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/felixrex82/operators-notes/main/public/uploads/1783933190234-ex1lapc0b84.jpg)


The good news is this doesn't require a two-hour morning ritual with candles and green juice. Ten minutes is enough to change what your brain's first input is. 

![Image](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/felixrex82/operators-notes/main/public/uploads/1783932752654-k0h9rxvrhd.png)

Thus, what actually matters is how easy you make it to reach the book before you reach the phone. If your phone is on the nightstand and the book is in a drawer somewhere, you already know which one wins. But if the book is open on a chair right beside your bed, and your phone is charging across the room, you've just changed the easiest option available the second you open your eyes.

Nobody rises into a blank day, you rise into whatever gets into your head first, and everything after that gets interpreted through it. A day that starts with somebody else's noise gets lived reactively while a day that starts with something you chose, something that asked your brain to actually focus, tends to get lived a little more on your own terms. It's such a small window — ten, fifteen minutes, tops but it's the one part of the day your brain is most open to being shaped by whatever shows up first. Most people hand that window to a notification but you don't have to.
