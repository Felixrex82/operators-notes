*You've sent many application letters, followed up some, politely — the way you were taught to, but then nothing happened. You checked your email more than a person should reasonably check anything, refreshed your bank app for a transfer you know isn't coming yet. You watch the visa portal say "processing" for the fortieth day in a row while somewhere in your family group chat, someone asks when you're "settling down" — the business, the wedding, the japa plans, doesn't matter which, the question always means the same thing: why hasn't this moved yet?*

Nothing is visibly happening. That's the part nobody prepares us for. Rejection at least gives you something to react to but the real test is the stretch before any answer arrives at all, where you've done what you can do and now the only thing left is to sit in it, with no idea how long "it" is.

Somewhere early, most of us picked up an assumption we never actually examined: that real effort should produce visible movement. When you send the CV you should get the job. When you post the product, you should get the customers. Do the work, feel the reward on a timeline you can sense, not one you have to take on faith. 

## Here's how we see things
School gave you a grade every term. A job gives you a payslip every month. Your phone gives you a notification the second anything happens anywhere, hence, you were trained your whole life on systems built to make progress observable in real time, but then comes *life*, and it refuses to follow that pattern.

> Life is straightly not linear 
- - Felixrex.

Now when the silence stretches — and it always stretches longer than feels reasonable — the mind doesn't say "this is just how timing works." It rather starts to think something is wrong with you specifically.

## What the Wait Actually Looked Like, Before Anyone Was Watching

Victor Osimhen's first professional season, at Wolfsburg in Germany, was sixteen appearances and zero goals. Not slow progress. *Absolute Zero*, and then a shoulder injury ended his season early. And then the two clubs he trialled with that summer chose not to sign him. Judging by any spreadsheet, nothing was happening to that career. Then Wolfsburg loaned him to Charleroi, a Belgian club most people outside football couldn't name, and he scored twenty goals in thirty-six games. 

He had said his confidence hit rock bottom right before that loan turned things around. The player who'd go on to become the most expensive African footballer in history, who'd lead Napoli to a league title they'd waited thirty-three years for, spent a year and a half looking from the outside, like a bust. 
He was in the part of the story that never gets filmed, because there's nothing to film.

Here is what's easy to miss about that stretch though: it wasn't him sitting still hoping someone would notice him. By his own account, the Charleroi loan is where his game actually got rebuilt — real minutes, real reps, real failure and correction, away from any spotlight. That distinction matters more than it sounds like it should. 
Thus, there's a version of waiting that's just enduring, arms folded, watching the clock. And there's a version where you're doing something with the time nobody's paying attention to yet. From the outside, both look exactly the same: silence, no scoreboard movement, nothing to post. From the inside, they're not the same thing at all, and only one of them tends to lead anywhere.

## Why the Stories You Compare Yourself To Never Mention This

Ask anyone to tell you how they got their win and listen to how much they compress. *"I kept applying, and then Company X reached out."* That sentence might be hiding fourteen months. *"We kept building, and then it just clicked."* That "and then" might be two years of a product nobody used. This isn't dishonesty. It's just how memory works once an outcome is known — the wait stops feeling like information and starts feeling like a footnote, so it gets cut from the telling.

Femi Otedola's rebuild after his 2009 collapse — the one where he went from courted billionaire to nearly ₦220 billion in debt, banks sending people to his house to recover it — didn't happen on a stage either. It happened through years of restructuring negotiations with AMCON, quiet and undramatic, before Forte Oil returned to real profit much later. 
Nobody filmed the meetings, the comeback got the headline. The years of paperwork and hard conversations that made the comeback possible didn't, because there's no glossy way to show a man slowly rebuilding his credibility with a bank.

You're not hearing any of that when you compare your own silence to someone else's highlight reel. You're hearing the ending of a story whose middle got edited out, and measuring your raw experience which is all you currently have — against their edited version. 
Of course it looks like they moved faster than you but every wait sounds fast once you already know it worked.

## The Part That's Actually Being Built

There's an old idea from the psychologist Graham Wallas, who spent time studying how people solve genuinely hard problems. After you've gathered everything you can and hit a wall, he noticed, there's a stage where nothing visibly happens — he called it incubation — before the actual insight, which he called illumination, eventually arrives.
The insight almost never shows up during the visible, effortful part. It shows up after the gap, and it's built on the gap, not separate from it.

You don't need psychology to know this, honestly — you already know it from garri. Cassava gets soaked and left to ferment for days before it becomes anything you can eat. Nobody stands over it telling it to hurry. You don't get to watch the fermentation working. You just know that if you rush it, what comes out isn't right. Your own waiting period tends to work the same way, if you let it. The version of you who sent fifty applications and heard nothing back isn't the same person who sent the first one. Something recalibrates in that silence — you notice the pattern in the rejections, you get sharper at the pitch, you stop wasting energy knocking on the wrong doors. None of that shows up on any scoreboard. All of it is real, and none of it happens without the wait.

## When It Isn't That

I don't want to make this sound tidier than it is, because waiting doesn't automatically do this work. Plenty of waiting is just delay with better PR. Refreshing a portal isn't incubation, hoping a situation resolves itself without you doing anything inside the silence isn't patience, even though it can feel virtuous in the moment. The difference isn't visible from outside — both versions look like the exact same nothing happening — but it matters enormously from inside, and you're usually the only one who can honestly tell which one you're in.

This same confusion follows people into their relationships, dressed up as "not being ready." Someone waits for a situationship to define itself, waits for a partner to come around, waits for feelings to arrive that would make a decision easy. Waiting can look like patience, and patience is supposed to be a virtue, so the waiting gets mistaken for wisdom. 
But ask what's actually happening inside it, is anything being built — an honest conversation, real clarity, a decision either way? Or is the relationship just sitting exactly where it sat six months ago, with both people hoping the silence itself will eventually answer the question neither of them will ask out loud? Being unsure is human but staying in an unresolved place indefinitely because clarity feels scarier than confusion is a different thing, and time alone rarely resolves it.

## The Question Worth Actually Asking

Everyone inside a waiting period asks the same thing, usually late at night: when will this end? It's the wrong question to spend your energy on, not because timing doesn't matter, but because you don't control the answer, and obsessing over a number that isn't yours to move just burns the one thing you do control — what you do with the time in between.

The quieter question is the useful one, probably because it's less satisfying: who am I becoming while this hasn't resolved yet? That part is entirely yours. Osimhen's sixteen scoreless games weren't erased by the twenty goals that followed — they were the ground the twenty goals grew out of. Otedola's restructuring years weren't a footnote before the real story. They were the real story; they just don't photograph.

*The waiting period isn't the pause before your life starts.* 
And for almost everyone, it turns out to be most of it — the actual bulk of the work, wearing the disguise of nothing happening. The people you admire didn't skip it. They just came out the other side of it before you started watching.
