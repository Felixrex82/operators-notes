When evaluating any business — yours or a competitor's — there's one question that cuts through everything else:

*What would it cost to replicate this in 18 months with $50M and a strong team?*

If the honest answer is "probably possible," you don't have a moat. You have a head start.

Head starts are valuable. Don't underestimate them. But they're not moats, and confusing the two is one of the more dangerous mistakes an operator can make.

## The Four Real Moats

After studying dozens of durable businesses, the actual moats compress into four:

**Network Effects**  
The product becomes more valuable as more people use it. The key insight most people miss: not all network effects are equal. Marketplace network effects (eBay) are weaker than communication network effects (WhatsApp) which are weaker than data network effects (Waze). Know which one you're building.

**Switching Costs**  
The cost — in time, money, or risk — of moving to a competitor is high enough that customers stay even when they're not fully satisfied. Enterprise software understands this better than anyone. Your CRM doesn't just hold data. It holds eight years of custom workflows, integrations, and institutional memory.

**Brand**  
Not logos. Not taglines. The felt sense that using a particular product says something true about who you are or what you value. Ferrari. Patagonia. Arc browser. These companies have pricing power that pure utility can't explain.

**Proprietary Data or Technology**  
A dataset that can't be replicated, or a technological capability that's genuinely difficult to match. This is rare, which is why most "tech" companies are actually distribution companies.

## What Most Startups Actually Have

Most early-stage startups don't have moats. What they have is:

- A better product (temporary advantage)
- A faster team (temporary advantage)
- Better distribution for their moment (temporary advantage)
- A specific customer relationship (meaningful, but not a moat)

None of these are bad. All of them are real competitive advantages. But none of them are durable without being converted into one of the four real moats.

The operator's job is to use the head start to build the moat.

## The Compounding Question

The most useful reframe I've found: instead of asking "what's our moat?" ask "what compounds?"

What gets better every day you operate?

Data compounds. Brand compounds. Customer trust compounds. Network effects compound. Distribution relationships compound.

Features don't compound. Marketing spend doesn't compound. Head count doesn't compound.

Find the thing in your business that compounds. Build everything around making that thing compound faster.

That's the moat.
