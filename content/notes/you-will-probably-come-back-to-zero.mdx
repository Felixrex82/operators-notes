- *There's a specific kind of quietness that shows up after everything falls apart. Not the loud kind, with tears and phone calls, it's the other kind; the Tuesday afternoon kind, when the business has folded, or the relationship is over, or the contract you were counting on just fell through, and you're sitting there trying to figure out what a person is even supposed to do now.*


Nobody tells you this moment is coming. You were promised a staircase but now you get a trapdoor instead.

And in that moment, most people quietly decide they've failed at life. Not because anything unusual has happened to them — this happens to almost everyone, even more than once, but because no one ever told them it would. 

You thought you were the only one who fell through the floor. You weren't, you just never see anyone else's floor giving way.

Here's what I want you to actually sit with, because it's the whole point of this piece: going back to zero is not a malfunction. It's not evidence that you did something wrong, or that you're cursed, or behind. It's the design. The system — life, this life, the one you're actually living, not the one on your timeline — *is built to reset you and repeatedly so,* and this is regardless of how far you've climbed, regardless of what you've built. That's not a bug, it's how it runs for almost everybody who's ever done anything worth doing.

> Life is straightly not linear
- - Felixrex

## The Timeline Nobody Agreed To

Somewhere along the way, without anyone actually promising it, you absorbed a schedule. By 22, you should have direction, by 26; a momentum and by 32; you should have arrived — house, career, partner, some version of "I've figured it out." The truth is that nobody handed you this timeline. You built it out of other people's highlight reels, and then you started grading yourself against it like it was real.

Open any app and you'll see it confirmed everywhere: the guy who "japa'd" and now posts from Canada like life is a straight line up, the couple who just did their wedding owambe and look settled forever, the boy who's suddenly "into crypto" and driving something new. 

It looks like proof that the timeline is real and you're the exception who's behind. But you're not looking at a fair sample of how life goes. You're looking at people's best three seconds, and best three seconds are, by definition, not the whole footage.

You only ever see the plane that landed. Nobody posts the business that shut down quietly, or the marriage that ended without a public statement, or the guy who's tried three different hustles and is still figuring out the fourth. Not because those things are rare, infact, they're not — but because there's no glossy way to package them. So your timeline isn't actually lying to you, it's just showing you the ones who made it back this time.

And the more of those you scroll past, the more your brain quietly starts treating early, easy success as the default — even though it's the exception. That's just how the mind works: it judges what's "normal" by what comes to mind easily, and if your phone feeds you twenty wins before breakfast, your brain files early arrival under "normal," even though, statistically, it almost never is.

## What Actually Tends to Happen

Here's the part that might surprise you, because it's the opposite of what the internet is selling you.

*Look at the people you actually respect — not the ones trending this week, the ones who are still standing ten, fifteen years later. Dangote didn't become "Dangote" in his twenties. The sugar and cement businesses that made him one of the richest men on the continent were built and rebuilt over decades, through crashes, through debt, through years nobody was filming. Femi Otedola was once nearly ₦220 billion in debt — banks sent people to his house to recover money, he lost properties, jets, shares, almost everything — after his fuel business, Zenon, got caught in the 2008 oil crash and the naira devaluation that followed. He has said openly that it felt like there was no waking up from it. That was not the end of his story. It was the middle of it. He rebuilt, restructured with AMCON, and today chairs one of Nigeria's biggest financial institutions.*

Or take someone from music instead of money. Burna Boy spent years being the guy who was clearly talented but somehow wasn't "there yet" — years where people his age were breaking out and he was still grinding, still being compared unfavorably to his mates. It took nearly a decade of work most people never saw before "African Giant" and the run that followed turned him into the biggest thing to come out of Nigerian music globally. Nobody posts the years of "not yet." Everybody posts the stadium.

This pattern isn't unique to Nigeria's billionaires and superstars either, it shows up in the actual data. Economists went digging through U.S. Census records a few years back to find out how old the founders of the fastest-growing companies in the country really were when they started out. 
Everyone assumes it's twenty-somethings in a garage. It wasn't. The average founder behind the highest-growth companies was 45 years old when they started. A fifty-year-old founder was almost twice as likely to build something huge as a thirty-year-old was. The thing that predicted success wasn't youth or hustle — it was years spent actually inside the industry first, failing, adjusting, learning what the market punishes and what it rewards.

Put plainly: the stability everyone is racing to have by 25 or 30 is, for most people who eventually get it, actually a 40s-and-beyond thing. Not because you're slow. Because that's roughly how long it takes anybody to gather enough scar tissue and pattern recognition to stop guessing. 
So, if you haven't "figured it out" by 30, you're not behind schedule, you're on the only schedule that was ever real.

## Two Different Kinds of Zero

Here's where I want you to slow down, because this is the part that actually matters, and most people get it backwards.

When your life falls apart, two different things can go to zero, and people usually only notice one of them. 
There's the number — your account balance, your business, your relationship status, the visible scoreboard everyone else can see. And then there's the other thing, the thing that doesn't show up on any scoreboard: your judgment, your instincts, your street sense, the thousand small calibrations you've made about what works and who you can trust and what a bad deal smells like before it fully goes bad.

The scoreboard resets constantly. It's the most common thing in the world. The second thing almost never resets — and that's the part people forget in the panic of watching the first thing crash.

Otedola's ₦220 billion debt didn't erase what he knew about running a fuel distribution business under pressure. If anything, it sharpened it — he's said the collapse forced him to reflect, reset, and rebuild with far more discipline than he had before. 
That's not a small consolation prize. That's the actual asset. 

The trader in Alaba who loses an entire container's worth of stock to a bad shipment still knows, in his gut, exactly what a bad supplier looks like before he signs the next deal. He didn't lose that lesson. He paid full price for it, and it's still his.

So when the account balance says zero and your head is spinning, the honest question isn't "how did I fail." It's "what actually went to zero here — and what didn't?" Almost always, the answer is: less than it feels like in the moment.

## Your Twenties and Thirties Were Never the Finished Product

*If your twenties and thirties aren't the arrival everyone promised, what are they actually for?*

They're for trying things that don't work, and being allowed to survive that without it meaning something is wrong with you. That's the whole assignment. Nobody arrives fully formed. The version of a person you're impressed by now — the aunty who now owns three properties, the uncle who "made it" in oil and gas, the friend who finally landed the role abroad — almost never got there in one clean shot. You just met them after the reset, not during it.

The discomfort of not having it figured out at 27, or 33, or even 40, isn't a sign you're doing life wrong. It's just what being in the middle of a long process feels like from the inside, before you can see how it turns out. You don't get the ending in advance. You only ever get the next decision, made without proof it's the right one.

## Stop Waiting to "Have It Figured Out" Before You Let Someone In.

This same broken timeline quietly wrecks how people think about relationships. You've probably said some version of it yourself: "I'm not settling down until I've figured myself out." It sounds mature. Half the time it's actually just fear wearing agbada — a way of postponing a real risk by demanding a level of certainty nobody has ever actually had.

So ask yourself honestly: who exactly has it figured out? The CEO is still adjusting strategy in her fifties. The pastor is still wrestling with questions he doesn't preach about. The retired civil servant spends the first year of retirement quietly lost, because the job that used to answer "who am I" is suddenly gone. Nobody finishes this. It's not a door you walk through once and lock behind you. It's more like weather — always there in the background, sometimes calm, sometimes not, never fully gone.

This isn't an argument for being reckless about who you commit to. There's a real difference between being genuinely careful — knowing your patterns, choosing well, not dragging someone into your unfinished business — and just waiting indefinitely for a certainty that isn't coming, using "responsibility" as the cover story. Waiting for a guarantee before you let someone matter to you isn't caution. It's fear, dressed nicer.

## What Actually Gets Rewarded

The internet rewards arrival. The launch, the wedding photos, the new car, the milestone. That's what gets the likes and the "God when."

Life rewards something else entirely: the ability to take a real hit, figure out what's actually gone versus what only feels gone, and start building again with whatever's still standing. Nobody's born knowing how to do that. You learn it exactly once — the first time you go through a real collapse and discover, usually to your own surprise, that most of you was still there afterward.

So maybe the point was never to build a life that avoids going back to zero. I don't think that life exists, no matter how convincingly people stage it online, and no matter how big the name is — Dangote has been to zero, Otedola has been to zero, plenty of people you admire have been to zero, some of them more than once. The more useful thing (the thing actually within your control) is becoming someone who knows how to rebuild. Someone who's already proven to themselves, at least once, that circumstances can be taken away a lot easier than character can.

Life is going to reset your account balance to zero a couple of times, maybe even your job title, or maybe your address, or who you get to do life with. What it almost never resets — if you paid attention on the way down — is the person all of that was busy turning you into. That was never the emergency. That was the plan.

Let me give you a quote to think on;

> "Life can only be understood backwards, but it must be lived forward."

- - Soren Kierkegaard
